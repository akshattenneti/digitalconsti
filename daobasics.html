Introduction to DAOs

Understanding the intersection of constitutional principles and decentralized governance

For Constitutional Law Professors: This guide explores how DAOs represent a novel application of constitutional principles in digital spaces. Throughout this resource, we'll highlight parallels between traditional constitutional frameworks and DAO governance.

Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) represent one of the most significant innovations in organizational governance since the development of the modern corporation. For constitutional law experts, DAOs offer a fascinating laboratory for observing how fundamental principles of governance—checks and balances, separation of powers, representation, and rights protection—are being reimagined in digital communities.

Just as constitutional framers sought to design systems that would balance efficiency with fairness, centralization with distribution of power, DAOs are creating new models for collective decision-making and resource allocation that challenge traditional organizational structures.

"DAOs represent a radical experiment in governance, asking the same questions that occupied Madison, Hamilton, and Jefferson: How do we create systems where power is appropriately distributed, rights are protected, and collective action is possible?"

— Professor Lawrence Lessig, Harvard Law School

In this guide, we'll explore the fundamentals of DAOs, examine their governance structures, provide tools for designing your own DAO, and analyze the legal and constitutional implications of these new organizational forms. Along the way, we'll provide interactive tools to help you understand how DAOs work and how they might be applied in various contexts.

Whether you're a constitutional scholar interested in new governance models, a legal practitioner advising clients on DAO formation, or an educator seeking to understand how constitutional principles are evolving in the digital age, this guide will provide you with the knowledge and tools to navigate the emerging world of decentralized governance.

DAO Fundamentals

Core concepts and elements that define Decentralized Autonomous Organizations

What is a DAO?

Constitutional Parallel

Similar to how constitutions define the structure, powers, and limitations of government, DAOs establish the fundamental rules and processes for decentralized communities.

A Decentralized Autonomous Organization (DAO) is an organization represented by rules encoded as a computer program that is transparent, controlled by its members, and not influenced by a central authority. The rules and transaction records of a DAO are maintained on a blockchain.

Key characteristics of DAOs include:

1

Decentralization

Unlike traditional organizations with hierarchical management structures, DAOs distribute decision-making power among their members. This creates a more democratic approach to governance where authority doesn't rest with a single individual or small group.

2

Transparency

All rules, decisions, and financial transactions in a DAO are recorded on a public blockchain, making them visible and verifiable to all members and often to the public. This creates unprecedented transparency in organizational governance.

3

Autonomy

Once established, DAOs operate according to their encoded rules with minimal human intervention. Smart contracts automatically execute decisions that have been approved through the governance process.

4

Member-owned

Participants in a DAO typically hold governance tokens that give them ownership stakes and voting rights proportional to their holdings. This creates direct alignment between participation and influence.

Example: MakerDAO

MakerDAO is one of the most well-established DAOs in the cryptocurrency space. It governs the Maker Protocol, which manages the DAI stablecoin. MakerDAO token holders vote on critical parameters such as stability fees, debt ceilings, and collateral types. This collective governance ensures that DAI maintains its peg to the US dollar through decentralized decision-making rather than central control.

Constitutional Perspective

Just as constitutional systems evolved from monarchies to representative democracies, DAOs represent an organizational evolution from centralized corporate structures to community-governed entities. Both transitions involve redistributing power and creating new mechanisms for collective decision-making.

Use Cases & Applications

DAOs are being applied across numerous domains, demonstrating their flexibility as a governance model:

Investment DAOs

Collective investment vehicles where members pool capital and vote on investment decisions. Examples include MetaCartel Ventures and The LAO.

Constitutional Relevance: Creating systems for collective financial decision-making with appropriate checks against majority overreach.

Creator & Media DAOs

Organizations funding creative work and managing content platforms. Examples include Bankless DAO and Friends With Benefits.

Constitutional Relevance: Balancing editorial independence with community governance and protecting freedom of expression.

Protocol DAOs

Governing decentralized applications and blockchain protocols. Examples include Uniswap and Compound.

Constitutional Relevance: Creating governance for public infrastructure with considerations for both users and developers.

Social Impact DAOs

Organizations focused on philanthropy and social good. Examples include Gitcoin and Klima DAO.

Constitutional Relevance: Creating governance for allocating resources toward public goods and social welfare.

Service DAOs

Providing professional services in a decentralized manner. Examples include dOrg and LexDAO.

Constitutional Relevance: Creating governance for professional guilds with quality control and dispute resolution.

Collector DAOs

Pooling resources to acquire and manage valuable assets. Examples include PleasrDAO and Constitution DAO.

Constitutional Relevance: Creating governance for collective ownership and stewardship of assets.

Case Study: Constitution DAO

In 2021, Constitution DAO formed rapidly to bid on a rare copy of the U.S. Constitution at Sotheby's. Within days, the DAO raised over $40 million from thousands of contributors. While ultimately outbid, this case demonstrates how DAOs can quickly organize collective action around shared objectives.

From a constitutional law perspective, this case illustrates how DAOs enable new forms of association for specific purposes—similar to how the First Amendment protects the right of citizens to assemble and petition.

Technology Behind DAOs

Understanding the technological components of DAOs helps clarify how they function and how they differ from traditional organizations:

1

Blockchain

The foundational technology that provides the transparent, tamper-resistant ledger on which DAO operations are recorded. Most DAOs operate on the Ethereum blockchain, though others use alternative blockchain platforms.

Constitutional Parallel: Like a public record of governmental proceedings, blockchain provides a permanent, accessible record of DAO activities.

2

Smart Contracts

Self-executing code that enforces the rules of the DAO. These contracts automatically implement decisions once they've been approved through the governance process. In essence, smart contracts are the "operating system" of a DAO.

Constitutional Parallel: Similar to how constitutional provisions establish the rules and procedures for government operations, smart contracts encode the fundamental rules of a DAO.

3

Governance Tokens

Digital assets that represent voting power and ownership within the DAO. Token holders typically have voting rights proportional to their holdings, though some DAOs implement alternative voting mechanisms.

Constitutional Parallel: Comparable to voting rights in a democracy, though with the distinction that influence can be proportional to token holdings rather than the one-person-one-vote principle.

4

Treasury Management

Systems for controlling the collective assets of the DAO. These typically include multi-signature wallets (requiring multiple approvals for transactions) and other security measures.

Constitutional Parallel: Similar to how governments establish treasuries and processes for appropriation, DAOs create systems for managing collective resources.

5

Governance Platforms

User interfaces and tools that facilitate participation in DAO governance, such as Snapshot for off-chain voting and Aragon for DAO management.

Constitutional Parallel: Like the development of electoral systems and legislative procedures in constitutional democracies, these platforms create the practical infrastructure for governance.

Important Concept: Instead of using terms like "multisig wallet" or "Gnosis Safe," we can understand these technologies as systems of shared responsibility. Rather than a single person controlling funds or making decisions, a DAO creates requirements for multiple approvals that distribute power and create checks and balances.

DAO Governance

Principles, models, and mechanisms for decentralized decision-making

Governance Models

DAOs employ various governance models, each with distinct characteristics and trade-offs. These models often blend elements from different traditions of political and organizational governance.

Token-Based Voting

The most common DAO governance model, where voting power is proportional to token holdings. One token equals one vote.

Common Simple
Constitutional Consideration

Similar to how early democracies often restricted voting rights to property owners, token-based voting creates a system where influence is tied to economic stake. This raises questions about plutocracy versus democracy in governance.

Quadratic Voting

A system where voting power scales with the square root of tokens held, reducing the influence of large token holders and creating more equitable decision-making.

Advanced Equitable
Constitutional Consideration

Quadratic voting attempts to balance the influence of wealth with the principle of broader participation, similar to how constitutional systems evolved to expand suffrage while maintaining stability.

Reputation-Based

Governance power is earned through contributions to the DAO and isn't transferable. Reputation systems reward participation and expertise rather than financial investment.

Advanced Complex
Constitutional Consideration

Reputation-based systems mirror meritocratic principles in governance, creating pathways for influence based on contribution rather than pre-existing advantage.

Holacratic/Role-Based

Decision-making authority is distributed across defined roles and circles (teams) with specific domains of authority. This creates autonomous workgroups within a broader structure.

Advanced Structured
Constitutional Consideration

Similar to federalist principles of distributed and enumerated powers, role-based governance creates defined jurisdictions of authority within a unified framework.

Futarchy

A governance system where members vote on values/outcomes, but the specific policies to achieve those outcomes are selected through prediction markets.

Experimental Complex
Constitutional Consideration

Futarchy attempts to separate value judgments (what we want) from empirical predictions (how to get there), creating a novel approach to the age-old constitutional challenge of combining popular will with effective implementation.

Voting Mechanisms

DAOs implement various voting systems to facilitate decision-making. These mechanisms balance efficiency, security, and participation:

On-Chain Voting

Votes are submitted directly to the blockchain, making them immutable and automatically executed through smart contracts.

Example

Compound governance allows token holders to submit and vote on proposals directly on the Ethereum blockchain. When a proposal passes, it automatically executes the associated code changes without requiring further human intervention.

Constitutional Parallel

On-chain voting creates a system where decisions, once made by the community, are automatically implemented—similar to how constitutional democracies establish procedures for laws to be enacted once they've passed through the required process.

Advantages: Secure, transparent, automatically executed

Disadvantages: Can be expensive (gas fees), less flexible

Off-Chain Voting

Votes are collected outside the blockchain (to save on transaction costs) but are cryptographically verifiable. Implementation typically requires a separate transaction.

Example

Snapshot is a popular off-chain voting platform that allows DAOs to create proposals and collect votes without incurring blockchain transaction fees. The votes are cryptographically signed to ensure authenticity, but implementation of decisions requires separate on-chain transactions.

Constitutional Parallel

Off-chain voting is comparable to advisory referendums or consultations that gauge public opinion but require additional steps to implement—creating a distinction between the expression of collective will and its execution.

Advantages: Cost-efficient, accessible, flexible

Disadvantages: Requires separate implementation, potential security concerns

Delegated Voting (Liquid Democracy)

Allows members to delegate their voting power to representatives who vote on their behalf. Unlike traditional representative democracy, delegation can be changed at any time and can be specific to certain types of decisions.

Example

The ENS DAO allows token holders to delegate their voting power to community members who are active and knowledgeable about specific issues. Delegates build reputations based on their voting history and community contributions.

Constitutional Parallel

Delegated voting creates a flexible representative system that attempts to blend direct and representative democracy, similar to how constitutional systems have evolved to include both direct democratic elements (referendums) and representative structures.

Advantages: Scalable, enables specialization, reduces voter apathy

Disadvantages: Can lead to power concentration, requires trust

Conviction Voting

Voting power accumulates over time as members maintain their vote for a proposal. This system rewards patience and long-term commitment over short-term mobilization.

Example

1Hive uses conviction voting for allocating community funds. Members signal support for proposals, and their voting power accumulates over time. This prevents sudden shifts in direction based on temporary enthusiasm or manipulation.

Constitutional Parallel

Conviction voting attempts to address the constitutional challenge of balancing responsiveness with stability, creating mechanisms that reward sustained support rather than momentary majorities.

Advantages: Resilient to manipulation, rewards commitment

Disadvantages: Complex, slower decision-making

Rage Quitting

A mechanism that allows members to exit the DAO with their proportional share of assets if they disagree with a decision. This creates a powerful check against majority overreach.

Example

Moloch DAO pioneered the rage quit mechanism, allowing members who disagree with a funding proposal to exit with their share of the treasury before the proposal is executed. This protects minority rights and creates incentives for consensus.

Constitutional Parallel

Rage quitting creates a form of minority protection akin to constitutional protections for individual rights. It provides an exit option that constrains majority power and encourages consensus-seeking.

Advantages: Protects minority rights, encourages consensus

Disadvantages: Can destabilize DAOs, potential for strategic threats

DAO Constitutions

Many DAOs establish constitution-like documents that articulate their fundamental values, governance processes, and decision-making procedures. These documents serve functions similar to governmental constitutions:

1

Establishing Fundamental Values

DAO constitutions typically begin by articulating the core values and mission of the organization. These foundational principles guide all other aspects of governance and operation.

Example: MakerDAO Declaration of Independence

"MakerDAO is an unbiased platform that provides the service of a decentralized currency and risk transfer mechanism for all users, without discrimination."

2

Defining Governance Processes

The constitution outlines how proposals are made, debated, voted on, and implemented. It establishes the "legislative process" of the DAO.

Example: Governance Process

"Proposals must be posted for discussion at least 7 days before voting begins. Voting periods last 3 days. Proposals require a 60% majority and at least 10% of tokens participating to pass."

3

Establishing Roles and Responsibilities

Many DAO constitutions define specific roles within the organization, such as core contributors, delegates, or specialized committees, along with their authorities and limitations.

Example: Role Definition

"The Protocol Engineering Core Unit is responsible for smart contract development and security. The core unit has autonomy in technical implementation but major protocol changes require DAO-wide approval."

4

Amendment Procedures

Like governmental constitutions, DAO constitutions typically establish procedures for their own amendment, often requiring supermajorities or extended deliberation.

Example: Amendment Process

"Changes to this constitution require a 75% majority vote with at least 20% of all tokens participating. Constitutional amendments must undergo a 14-day discussion period before voting."

5

Dispute Resolution

Many DAO constitutions establish mechanisms for resolving conflicts between members or interpreting constitutional provisions.

Example: Dispute Resolution

"In case of disputes over the interpretation of this constitution, a Judicial Committee of 7 members elected by the DAO shall provide a binding interpretation after hearing arguments from all sides."

Constitutional Law Parallel

The development of DAO constitutions mirrors the process of constitutional drafting in nation-states. Both involve establishing foundational rules for governance, procedures for collective decision-making, and mechanisms for resolving disputes and adapting to changing circumstances.

However, DAO constitutions often place greater emphasis on exit rights (the ability to leave with one's assets) as a check on collective power, whereas state constitutions typically focus more on voice (representation and rights protection) as citizens cannot easily exit their jurisdictions.

Treasury Management

DAOs require mechanisms for managing collective resources. This involves both security measures and governance processes for resource allocation:

Instead of Technical Jargon: Rather than discussing "multisig wallets" or "Gnosis Safe," we'll focus on the constitutional principles behind secure treasury management: distributed control, checks and balances, and transparent accounting.
1

Distributed Control

Most DAOs implement systems requiring multiple approvers for any transaction over specified thresholds. This creates a system of checks and balances where no single individual can unilaterally control funds.

Example

A DAO might require that 3 out of 5 elected treasury guardians approve any transaction over $10,000, creating distributed responsibility and preventing unilateral control.

Constitutional Parallel

Similar to how constitutional systems separate powers related to public finances (e.g., requiring both legislative appropriation and executive implementation), DAOs create distributed control over treasury functions.

2

Funding Allocation Mechanisms

DAOs develop processes for allocating resources to projects, contributors, or investments. These can range from direct proposal voting to more sophisticated systems.

Example: Gitcoin Quadratic Funding

Gitcoin uses quadratic funding for grants, where the number of contributors matters more than the amount contributed. This creates a democratic approach to funding allocation that balances small and large donors.

Constitutional Parallel

The challenge of fairly allocating communal resources is central to both constitutional governance and DAO treasury management, with both developing procedural solutions to prevent capture by special interests.

3

Budgeting Systems

Many DAOs implement budgeting processes that provide predictable funding to working groups or contributors without requiring case-by-case approval for every expense.

Example: Core Unit Budgets

MakerDAO allocates quarterly budgets to Core Units (specialized teams) who then have autonomy to manage those funds for their mandated activities, creating a balance between oversight and operational flexibility.

Constitutional Parallel

Like governmental budgeting processes that balance legislative oversight with executive discretion, DAO budgeting systems create accountability while allowing operational autonomy.

4

Transparency and Accounting

DAOs typically implement systems for transparent recording and reporting of all financial activities, allowing members to monitor resource use.

Example: On-Chain Transparency

Due to blockchain transparency, all DAO transactions are publicly visible and immutable. Many DAOs supplement this with regular financial reporting and dashboards that make the information more accessible.

Constitutional Parallel

Transparency in public finance is a key principle in constitutional governance, with DAOs taking this to a new level through blockchain-based verification and real-time visibility.

Legal & Constitutional Aspects

Understanding the legal context and constitutional principles underlying DAOs

Legal Challenges

DAOs present novel legal challenges due to their borderless, decentralized nature and lack of fit with existing legal frameworks.

1

Legal Personhood

Traditional organizations typically have legal personhood—the ability to enter contracts, own property, and engage in legal proceedings. DAOs often lack this status, creating challenges for formal legal interactions.

Constitutional Parallel

The question of who has legal personhood has evolved throughout constitutional history, from excluding certain groups to expanding recognition. DAOs represent a new frontier in this evolution.

2

Liability

Without a formal legal structure, DAO members may face unlimited personal liability for the organization's actions and debts—a significant risk compared to structures like corporations that offer liability protection.

Example: The DAO Hack

The original DAO project in 2016 was exploited for approximately $50 million due to a smart contract vulnerability. This raised questions about who would be liable for losses and how recovery would be handled without traditional legal structures.

3

Regulatory Compliance

DAOs often operate in regulatory gray areas, potentially falling under securities laws, financial regulations, data protection requirements, and other frameworks that weren't designed for decentralized organizations.

Constitutional Parallel

The tension between innovation and regulation mirrors constitutional debates about the proper scope of governmental authority and the balance between freedom and order.

4

Jurisdictional Questions

DAOs typically operate globally, raising complex questions about which jurisdictions' laws apply and how conflicting requirements should be reconciled.

Example: MakerDAO

MakerDAO operates globally, with contributors and users across dozens of countries. This creates challenges in determining which data protection laws, securities regulations, and other requirements apply to its operations.

5

Contract Enforcement

While DAOs use smart contracts for automatic enforcement, these may not align with legal contract requirements, creating ambiguity about remedies if disputes arise.

Constitutional Parallel

The relationship between code-based rules and legal systems mirrors the constitutional question of ultimate sovereignty—whether final authority rests with formal law, social consensus, or other mechanisms.

Constitutional Principles in DAOs

DAOs embody several key constitutional principles that parallel those found in governmental systems:

Separation of Powers

Many DAOs implement structures that separate different types of authority, similar to how governmental constitutions separate legislative, executive, and judicial functions.

Constitutional Government DAO Equivalent Legislative (makes laws) Token holders voting on proposals Executive (implements laws) Working groups/committees executing approved proposals Judicial (interprets laws) Dispute resolution mechanisms, arbitration committees
Example: Compound's Governor Bravo

Compound's governance system separates proposal creation, voting, and implementation. Proposals must go through a formal process before being voted on by token holders, and once approved, they are implemented through a timelock mechanism that provides a delay before execution.

Constitutional Analysis

The separation of powers in DAOs serves the same purpose as in constitutional systems: preventing the concentration of authority and creating specialized functions that enable more effective governance.

Checks & Balances

DAOs implement various mechanisms that allow different elements of the organization to check and constrain each other's power.

Constitutional Government DAO Equivalent Presidential veto Multisig wallet (requiring multiple approvals) Judicial review Dispute resolution committees Congressional oversight Token holder review of committee activities Impeachment Removal votes for team members or delegates
Example: Moloch DAO's Rage Quit

Moloch DAO implemented the innovative "rage quit" mechanism that allows members to exit with their proportional share of assets if they disagree with a funding proposal. This creates a powerful check against majority overreach by giving the minority an exit option.

Constitutional Analysis

Like constitutional checks and balances, these mechanisms prevent tyranny of the majority and create incentives for consensus-building rather than winner-takes-all governance.

Representation

DAOs grapple with the same questions of representation that have shaped constitutional governance: who gets a voice in decisions and how is that voice expressed?

Constitutional Approach DAO Equivalent Direct democracy (referendums) All token holders voting on all proposals Representative democracy Delegate systems for voting Property requirements for voting Token-weighted voting (1 token = 1 vote) Universal suffrage One-member-one-vote systems
Example: ENS DAO Delegation

The ENS DAO implements a delegation system where token holders can delegate their voting power to community representatives. This creates a form of representative democracy within the DAO, allowing for specialized knowledge while maintaining democratic legitimacy.

Constitutional Analysis

DAOs are experimenting with both plutocratic systems (where influence scales with economic stake) and more egalitarian approaches—mirroring the historical evolution of voting rights in constitutional systems.

Rights Protections

Many DAOs establish fundamental rights for their members that cannot be easily overridden by majority vote—similar to constitutional bills of rights.

Constitutional Right DAO Equivalent Freedom of speech Right to propose and debate Property rights Ownership of tokens, right to exit with assets Due process Fair dispute resolution procedures Equal protection Non-discrimination in participation
Example: BanklessDAO Constitution

The BanklessDAO constitution explicitly protects members' rights to propose ideas, participate in governance, and access information about the DAO's operations—establishing procedural rights similar to those found in constitutional systems.

Constitutional Analysis

Like constitutional protections for individual rights, these mechanisms create areas where collective power is limited, establishing boundaries that even majority decisions cannot cross.

Amendment Process

DAOs establish procedures for changing their fundamental rules that typically require greater consensus than ordinary decisions—paralleling constitutional amendment processes.

Constitutional Amendment Process DAO Equivalent Supermajority requirements Higher voting thresholds for constitutional changes Ratification by states Multiple approval stages or stakeholder groups Extended deliberation periods Longer discussion timeframes for fundamental changes Constitutional conventions Special working groups for major governance revisions
Example: MakerDAO Governance Polls

MakerDAO distinguishes between regular decisions and changes to the core governance mechanisms. The latter require higher thresholds for approval and longer deliberation periods, creating a distinction between ordinary governance and "constitutional" changes.

Constitutional Analysis

These amendment procedures reflect the same balance sought in constitutional systems: allowing adaptation over time while ensuring that fundamental changes require broad consensus rather than narrow majorities.

Legal Structures for DAOs

To address the legal challenges of DAOs, several approaches have emerged to bridge decentralized governance with traditional legal frameworks:

DAO LLCs

Several jurisdictions have created specific legal structures for DAOs, beginning with Wyoming's DAO LLC legislation in 2021. These provide liability protection while accommodating decentralized governance.

Constitutional Parallel

The creation of new legal forms for DAOs mirrors how constitutional systems have evolved to recognize new types of entities and relationships over time.

Legal Wrappers

Many DAOs use traditional legal structures (foundations, LLCs, corporations) as "wrappers" that interact with the external world while being governed by the DAO internally.

Example: The LAO

The LAO is structured as a Delaware LLC with membership interests represented by tokens. This creates a bridge between traditional legal protections and decentralized governance.

Unincorporated Associations

Some DAOs operate as unincorporated associations—groups of individuals united for a common purpose without formal legal registration.

Risk

This approach typically leaves members with unlimited personal liability, similar to general partnerships in many jurisdictions.

Offshore Structures

Some DAOs incorporate in jurisdictions with favorable regulatory environments for blockchain projects, such as the Cayman Islands or Switzerland.

Constitutional Consideration

This approach raises questions about regulatory arbitrage and the proper balance between sovereignty and global cooperation.

Contract-Based Approaches

Some DAOs rely on contracts between members that define rights, responsibilities, and dispute resolution mechanisms without creating a formal legal entity.

Example: Operating Agreements

Members may sign operating agreements that recognize the DAO's governance while establishing legally enforceable relationships between participants.

Legal Disclaimer: This information is provided for educational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. The legal context for DAOs is rapidly evolving, and appropriate structures depend on specific circumstances and jurisdictions.

Jurisdiction & Enforcement

DAOs operate in a global context that challenges traditional notions of jurisdiction and enforcement. This creates both opportunities and complexities:

1

Jurisdictional Challenges

DAOs typically have members, assets, and operations spread across multiple jurisdictions, making it difficult to determine which laws apply and how they should be enforced.

Constitutional Parallel

This mirrors federalism questions in constitutional systems: how authority is allocated across different levels and regions, and how conflicts between jurisdictions are resolved.

2

Code vs. Law

DAOs operate primarily through code that executes automatically, potentially creating tensions with legal systems that may interpret or enforce rules differently.

Example: The DAO Fork

After The DAO hack in 2016, the Ethereum community implemented a hard fork to recover funds, despite the fact that the exploitation technically followed the rules of the smart contract. This highlighted the tension between code-as-law and community governance.

3

Enforcement Mechanisms

Without clear jurisdictional authority, DAOs rely on a combination of technical enforcement (through smart contracts), reputational incentives, and internal governance to ensure compliance with rules.

Constitutional Parallel

This reflects the fundamental constitutional question of what ultimately enforces the rules—formal authority, social consensus, economic incentives, or technological constraints.

4

Emerging Legal Frameworks

Various jurisdictions are developing approaches to DAO regulation, from specific recognition (as in Wyoming) to applying existing frameworks to new organizational forms.

Example: DAO Legislation

Wyoming's DAO LLC law created a specific legal structure for DAOs, while other jurisdictions are exploring how existing entity types and securities regulations apply to decentralized organizations.

5

Global Governance Questions

DAOs raise fundamental questions about how governance should work in a globally connected world where organizations and communities transcend national boundaries.

Constitutional Parallel

This connects to longstanding constitutional debates about sovereignty, international law, and the proper scope of territorial jurisdiction in an interconnected world.

Case Studies

Examining real-world DAOs and their governance approaches

MakerDAO

Type: Protocol DAO (DeFi)

Purpose: Governing the Maker Protocol, which issues the DAI stablecoin

1

Governance Structure

MakerDAO has evolved from a relatively simple token voting system to a more complex structure with specialized Core Units (teams with specific responsibilities) and delegate voting.

Constitutional Parallel

MakerDAO's evolution mirrors how constitutional systems often develop more specialized institutions over time as they mature and face more complex governance challenges.

2

Decision-Making Process

MakerDAO uses a two-stage governance process: non-binding "Governance Polls" to gauge sentiment and build consensus, followed by binding "Executive Votes" that implement changes to the protocol.

Example

When making decisions about adding new collateral types for DAI, MakerDAO first conducts polls on the risk parameters, then finalizes the decision through an Executive Vote that actually implements the changes.

3

Core Units

MakerDAO has established specialized teams (Core Units) with defined mandates and budgets, including groups focused on governance, risk, protocol engineering, growth, and other functions.

Constitutional Parallel

This structure resembles the development of specialized administrative agencies in constitutional governments, creating domain expertise while maintaining overall democratic accountability.

4

Constitutional Principles

MakerDAO's governance embodies several constitutional principles:

  • Separation of powers between proposal creation, deliberation, and implementation
  • Checks and balances through the Emergency Shutdown mechanism and delegate oversight
  • Representative governance through the delegate system
  • Amendment procedures that require greater consensus for fundamental changes

ENS DAO

Type: Protocol DAO (Naming Service)

Purpose: Governing the Ethereum Name Service protocol

1

Governance Structure

ENS DAO uses a delegate system where token holders can delegate their voting power to community representatives. The DAO oversees both technical direction and treasury management.

Example

ENS token holders can either vote directly or delegate their voting power to active community members who have built reputations for expertise and engagement.

2

Constitution

ENS DAO established a formal constitution outlining core principles and governance processes. The constitution includes provisions for amendments, dispute resolution, and the roles of various stakeholders.

Constitutional Parallel

The ENS Constitution serves the same function as governmental constitutions—establishing fundamental rules, values, and procedures that guide all other governance activities.

3

Working Groups

ENS DAO established specialized working groups (meta-governance, social, technical, finance) to focus on specific aspects of the protocol's development and governance.

Constitutional Parallel

This structure resembles the development of specialized committees in legislative bodies, combining expertise with democratic oversight.

4

Constitutional Principles

ENS DAO's governance embodies several constitutional principles:

  • Representative democracy through the delegate system
  • Checks and balances between working groups and the broader community
  • Public goods provision by managing a critical internet infrastructure
  • Amendment procedures for updating the constitution itself

Purpose: Governing the Uniswap decentralized exchange protocol

Governance Structure

Uniswap governance uses a proposal-based system where UNI token holders can propose and vote on changes to the protocol. The system includes delegation capabilities and a timelock mechanism for implementation.

Example

Proposals to adjust the protocol fee or deploy to new blockchains go through a formal proposal process, discussion period, and voting before implementation.

2

Treasury Management

The Uniswap DAO controls a substantial treasury used to fund development, liquidity incentives, and other initiatives that benefit the protocol.

Constitutional Parallel

The management of the Uniswap treasury mirrors public finance processes in constitutional systems, requiring collective decision-making about resource allocation.

Delegation System

Constitutional Parallel

This delegation system parallels representative democracy in constitutional systems, allowing for specialized knowledge while maintaining democratic legitimacy.

Constitutional Principles

Uniswap's governance embodies several constitutional principles:

  • Proposal rights for stakeholders with sufficient token holdings
  • Deliberative processes through discussion periods
  • Timelock mechanisms that create cooling-off periods before implementation

Constitution DAO

Type: Collector DAO

Purpose: Formed to purchase a rare copy of the U.S. Constitution at auction

1

Rapid Formation

Constitution DAO formed quickly in response to a specific opportunity—the auction of a rare copy of the U.S. Constitution. This demonstrated the ability of DAOs to mobilize resources rapidly for specific purposes.

Example

Within days, the DAO raised over $40 million from thousands of contributors, showing the potential for coordinating collective action without traditional organizational structures.

2

Mission-Focused Governance

The DAO had a narrow, specific mission—to purchase the document at auction and determine its future governance. This clear purpose facilitated rapid decision-making despite the lack of elaborate governance structures.

Constitutional Parallel

This resembles the formation of special-purpose governmental entities or commissions with clear mandates and limited scope.

3

Dissolution Challenges

After being outbid at auction, the DAO faced the challenge of unwinding—returning funds to contributors or pivoting to a new purpose. This highlighted the need for clear processes for organizational dissolution or transformation.

Constitutional Parallel

The dissolution challenges mirror questions in constitutional systems about how to handle transitions, succession, and institutional change.

4

Legacy

Despite not achieving its primary goal, Constitution DAO demonstrated the potential for DAOs to mobilize collective action around shared interests, particularly for public goods or culturally significant assets.

Constitutional Parallel

This highlights how DAOs can facilitate new forms of association and collective action—similar to how constitutional rights of assembly and association enable group efforts for public purposes.

Guild Structure

BanklessDAO organized into specialized guilds (writers, developers, designers, etc.) that operate semi-autonomously within the broader organization. This creates a federal structure with distributed authority.

Example

The Writers Guild within BanklessDAO has its own processes for commissioning and editing content, while still operating within the broader DAO governance framework.

2

Seasonal Planning

BanklessDAO operates in "seasons" with specific goals, budgets, and initiatives for each period. This creates clear planning and evaluation cycles for the organization's activities.

Constitutional Parallel

This resembles legislative sessions or budget cycles in governmental systems, creating regular periods for evaluation and adjustment.

3

Forum-Based Governance

BanklessDAO uses an extensive forum system for proposal development and discussion, creating a deliberative process before formal voting takes place.

Constitutional Parallel

This forum structure resembles deliberative bodies in constitutional systems, emphasizing discussion and refinement before formal decision-making.

4

Constitutional Principles

BanklessDAO's governance embodies several constitutional principles:

  • Federalism through the guild structure
  • Deliberative democracy through extensive forum discussions
  • Regular evaluation cycles through the seasonal structure
  • Transparency through open documentation and reporting

Glossary of Terms

Key concepts and terminology for understanding DAOs and their governance

DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization)

An organization represented by rules encoded as a computer program that is transparent, controlled by its members, and not influenced by a central authority. The rules and transaction records are maintained on a blockchain.

Blockchain

A distributed, immutable ledger technology that records transactions across many computers so that the record cannot be altered retroactively without altering all subsequent blocks and the consensus of the network.

Smart Contract

Self-executing code on a blockchain that automatically implements the terms of an agreement when predefined conditions are met. Smart contracts form the operational foundation of most DAOs.

Governance Token

A cryptocurrency that represents voting power within a DAO. Holders can participate in governance decisions proportionally to their holdings.

Multisignature Wallet

A digital wallet that requires multiple private key signatures to authorize a transaction, creating distributed control over assets.

Quorum

The minimum level of participation required for a vote to be considered valid, typically expressed as a percentage of total possible votes.

Supermajority

A voting threshold higher than a simple majority (>50%), such as two-thirds (67%) or three-quarters (75%), typically required for significant decisions.

On-Chain Governance

Governance processes where voting and execution happen directly on the blockchain, providing maximum transparency and automatic implementation.

Off-Chain Governance

Governance processes where voting occurs outside the blockchain (to save costs), with results cryptographically verified and implemented separately.

Delegation

The process of transferring voting power to another address while retaining ownership of the underlying tokens, creating a representative governance system.

Quadratic Voting

A voting system where voting power scales with the square root of tokens held, reducing the influence of large token holders.

Conviction Voting

A voting system where voting power increases the longer a vote is maintained for a proposal, rewarding consistent, long-term support.

Rage Quit

A mechanism allowing DAO members to exit with their proportional share of assets if they disagree with a decision, protecting minority rights.

Timelock

A delay between when a proposal is approved and when it is implemented, providing time for review and emergency action if needed.

Governance Forum

An online platform where DAO members discuss proposals, governance issues, and other community matters before formal voting.

DAO Constitution

A document outlining the fundamental principles, values, governance processes, and amendment procedures for a DAO.

DAO LLC

A specific legal structure created by Wyoming legislation that provides limited liability protection for DAO members while accommodating decentralized governance.

Treasury Management

The processes and controls for managing a DAO's collective assets, including security measures and allocation procedures.

Token Economics

The design of incentives, distribution, and utility of tokens within a DAO ecosystem, shaping participation and governance.
